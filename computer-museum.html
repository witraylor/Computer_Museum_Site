Computer and Communications Museum of Ireland

Accessibility Review

The Computer and Communications Museum of Ireland contains early radios, iconic computers, and vintage games, including 'Space Invaders' and 'Pacman'. Visitors can can explore and experience the evolving technological landscape across the decades.

Galway: Ireland's First Digital City

Galway is Ireland’s first digital city, incorporating digital technology into its core infrastructure to address the emerging needs of society and improve thre sustainability of the built environment. In the 1970s, Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) was one of the world’s leading computer companies when it opened at Mervue its first manufacturing plant outside the USA. Its presence had a significant positive impact on Ireland’s exports as well as in helping to reverse Irish emigration and in transforming Galway city into a vibrant, cosmopolitan city with a global reputation for its skilled industrial workforce and scientific technological research sector.

Museum Accessibility Checklist:

Exhibits and Objects

Small objects are mostly at or below seated eye level. Display cases are shallow enough to allow for clear viewing from above. While objects are visible, some description cards are placed flat. This makes them difficult to read for anyone with a lower eye level; tilting these cards would improve readability for all visitors.

Labels and text

Overall, the contrast and fonts of all labels were well done and easy to read. However, there were issues with sizes at some points. The broad descriptions and historical stories were easy to decipher; it became extremely difficult with smaller image labels. There were also no options for visually impaired users to have an audio or braille option. The Egypt room text was nearly impossible to read as it was very dimly lit.

Audio visuals and interactive

Accessibility features in the museum were inconsistent, with some exhibits offering audio guides while others lacked accessibility indicators. Video displays also did not include captions, and interactive headphones appeared intended more for general use than accessibility support.

Pathway and Navigation

The museum space is quite tight, and wheelchair users would likely have trouble navigating the exhibit. Sections of the exhibit are also clearly marked with signage.

Seating and Furniture

The museum contains seating at the front and in front of larger screens.

Safety and Content

The museum's safety features are mostly accessible for those with disabilities. There are clearly marked exits that were also verbally explained at the start of the tour, and the fire alarms appear to have both light and sound. However, due to the cramped space, visitors with mobility disabilities may have trouble quickly exiting the museum.

What Students Learned and Enjoyed

Students learned a lot about the origins of telecommunications in Ireland, pioneering communicating across airwaves instead of using the telephone lines under the ocean.

Many students enjoyed playing the vintage games throughout the museum

They also enjoyed the phone booth that played music from different years.

Accessibility Features:

Most of the video games are low enough for a wheelchair user to access

There is an elevator on the main floor of the building to access the museum on the second floor.

Non-Accessible Features:

A lot of the vintage computer monitors flash, making it difficult to see the screen and potentially cause headaches or seizures.

Certain parts of the museum are not wide enough for a wheelchair user to navigate.
